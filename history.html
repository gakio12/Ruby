History

February 24, 1993 – Ruby is conceived by Yukihiro Matsumoto, in an online conversation with colleague Keiju Ishitsuka. While Ruby is named for a colleague’s birthstone, inspired by the use of a gemstone for Perl, he later realized the following comparisons:

  • In birthstones, pearl is June, ruby is July.
  • When measuring font sizes, pearl is 5pt, ruby is 5.5pt[1].
Ruby seemed a natural progression from Perl. In stating his inspiration for the language, “I wanted a scripting language that was more powerful than Perl, and more object-oriented than Python[2].”

December 21, 1995 – Ruby 0.95 released. This is the first public release of Ruby. FOR THE BRAVE: You can still download this release here.

December 25, 1996 – Ruby 1.0 released. Merry Christmas!

December 7, 1998 – A simple Ruby home page is launched in English[3].

October 27, 1999 – Matz and Keiju publish The Object-oriented Scripting Language Ruby in Japan, the first book available on Ruby[4].

October 2001 – The first English language book on Ruby, Programming Ruby, published. Written by David Thomas and Andrew Hunt. While Ruby had already surpassed Python’s popularity in Japan, this marked the first true growth outside of Japan.

February 16, 2002 – The volume of traffic on the English language mailing list ruby-talk surpasses that of ruby-list­, the original Japanese mailing list[5].

December 13, 2005 – Rails 1.0 released[6]. A framework built in Ruby, Ruby on Rails is commonly considered “Ruby’s biggest turning point”[7].

December 25, 2013 – Ruby 2.1 released. Merry Christmas![8]

December 25, 2014 – Ruby 2.2 released. Merry Christmas![9]

December 25, 2015 – Ruby 2.3 released. Merry Christmas![10]
